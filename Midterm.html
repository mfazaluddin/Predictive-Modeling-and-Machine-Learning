Looking at the elbow plot, 3 clusters appears to be the ideal choice. We can see a clear "bend" in the graph at K=3, after which adding more clusters doesn't give much benefit. Selecting K=3 captures key patterns in our data while keeping the analysis clear and meaningful.¶

# Create K means graph
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
from sklearn.cluster import KMeans

# Use 'Age' (X1) and 'Blood Type' (X2) for clustering
X1 = data['Age']
X2 = data['Blood Type']
X = data[['Age', 'Blood Type']].values

# Fit K-Means with 3 clusters
km = KMeans(n_clusters=3)
km.fit(X)
pred = km.predict(X)  # Predict the clusters

# Scatter plot of Age vs. Blood Type with clusters
plt.scatter(X1, X2, c=pred, s=50, cmap='spring')
centers = km.cluster_centers_  # Get the cluster centers

# Plot the cluster centers
plt.scatter(centers[:, 0], centers[:, 1], c='black', s=200, alpha=0.75, label='Centers')
plt.xlabel('Age')
plt.ylabel('Blood Type')
plt.title('K-Means Clustering (3 Clusters)')
plt.legend()
plt.show()

Accuracy: 0.125658

Looking at the K-means clustering results for different blood types across age groups, the visualization shows three distinct clusters (yellow, magenta, and pink) dividing people by age ranges: about 10-35, 35-60, and 60-90 years of age. But the low accuracy of about 12.56% shows us something – blood type and age are not correlated. This makes some amount of sense logically because blood type is a genetic trait and does not vary with age. The clusters that we see are just the division of people according to age intervals, but these intervals do not correlate with blood types, which is why the accuracy of the prediction is very low.¶
